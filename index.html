The Turtle Population of Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka is known for its beautiful beaches and crystal-clear waters, home to a diverse population of sea turtles. The island is located in the Indian Ocean, along the path of the turtle migration, making it an important nesting and breeding site for these ancient creatures. Unfortunately, the turtle populations in Sri Lanka are under severe threat, with their numbers declining rapidly due to various human activities and climate change. In this essay, we will explore the different types of sea turtles found in Sri Lanka, their habitats, the threats they face, and the conservation efforts being taken to protect and conserve them.

The Types Of Turtles

Sri Lanka is home to five species of sea turtles, making it a vital location for sea turtle conservation efforts. Each of these species plays a unique role in the marine ecosystem, from the Green Turtle's herbivorous diet to the Hawksbill Turtle's role in maintaining coral reef health.

  1. Green Turtle

  2. Hawksbill Turtle

  3. Loggerhead Turtle

  4. Leatherback Turtle

  5. Olive Ridley Turtle

The Green Turtle


The green turtle, scientifically known as Chelonia mydas, is a fascinating marine reptile that belongs to the family Cheloniidae. As their name suggests, they have a characteristic green coloration, particularly on their fat deposits and skin, although their shells are typically brown or olive in color. Green turtles are known for their large size, with adults reaching an average length of 3 to 4 feet (1 to 1.2 meters) and weighing around 200 to 500 pounds (90 to 230 kilograms).

The Hawksbill Turtle


The hawksbill turtle is a critically endangered species of sea turtle found in tropical and subtropical waters around the world, including Sri Lanka. They are named for their sharply pointed beak, which they use to feed on sponges and other invertebrates. Hawksbill turtles are smaller than other sea turtle species, with adults typically weighing between 100 and 200 pounds, and are unfortunately heavily hunted for their ornate carapace.

The Loggerhead Turtle


The loggerhead turtle is a species of sea turtle found in the Atlantic, Indian, and Pacific oceans, including Sri Lanka. It is named for its large head and strong jaw, which it uses to feed on hard-shelled prey such as crabs and mollusks. Loggerhead turtles are one of the largest sea turtle species, with adults weighing between 200 and 400 pounds, and like other sea turtles, they face a variety of threats including habitat loss, pollution, and accidental capture in fishing nets.

Our Hatcheries

Sri Lankan hatcheries are important conservation sites that aim to protect and conserve sea turtle populations, which have been threatened by a variety of human activities. These hatcheries, collect sea turtle eggs from the beach and bring them to the hatchery to protect them from predators and human disturbance.Sri Lankas three main turtle hatcheries are located in hikkaduwa, Kosgoda and Bentota.

Hikkaduwa

Hikkaduwa Turtle Hatchery is one of the oldest and most well-known turtle hatcheries in Sri Lanka. Located in the southern coastal town of Hikkaduwa, the hatchery plays a crucial role in protecting sea turtle populations in the area.

The hatchery collects sea turtle eggs from the beach and provides a safe environment for them to hatch and grow before releasing them back into the ocean. Visitors can also learn about sea turtles and their conservation through educational programs and interaction with the turtles themselves.

Kosgoda

Kosgoda Turtle Hatchery is another popular and important hatchery in Sri Lanka, located on the southwest coast. The hatchery was established in 1981 with the goal of protecting sea turtles from extinction. It collects eggs from the beach and brings them to the hatchery for incubation, ensuring their safety from predators and human disturbance.

The hatchery also releases hatched turtles into the ocean and provides education and awareness programs for the public to increase understanding of the importance of sea turtle conservation.

Bentota

Bentota Turtle Hatchery is a conservation site located in the coastal town of Bentota, Sri Lanka. Established in 1986, the hatchery aims to protect and conserve sea turtle populations through the collection and incubation of eggs.

Visitors can observe the hatchery's conservation efforts and learn about sea turtle biology and conservation through educational programs and interaction with the turtles themselves. The hatchery also releases hatched turtles into the ocean and works to increase awareness.

The Threats to the turtles

Sea turtles are facing a number of threats that are putting their survival in jeopardy. These threats range from habitat loss and pollution to overfishing and climate change. Sea turtles are also at risk of entanglement in fishing nets and other debris in the ocean, which can lead to injury or death. In addition, illegal poaching and egg collection continue to be major threats to sea turtle populations in many parts of the world. It is essential that we take action to address these threats and work towards the conservation and protection of these beloved marine creatures.

Climate Change

Climate change can affect sea turtles in a number of ways, including changes in ocean currents and sea level rise. Rising temperatures can also impact the sex ratio of sea turtle hatchlings, potentially leading to imbalances in populations.

Habitat Loss

Primarily due to coastal development and human activity, can have a devastating impact on sea turtle populations. Loss of nesting beaches and foraging areas can affect their ability to breed and feed, leading to declines in population size.

Overfishing

As sea turtles feed on jellyfish and other prey, overfishing can result in a decline in these species, leaving turtles with less to eat.And fishing gear can entangle and harm sea turtles, further contributing to their decline.
